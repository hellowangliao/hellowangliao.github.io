一段数学证明

Theorem Let $V$ be a vector space over the field $F$ and let $\mathscr{C}$ be a collection of finite number of subspaces of $V$. If the number of the subspaces in $\mathscr{C}$ is not more that the cardinality of $F$ when $F$ is finite or $F$ is infinite, then the union of all the elements in $\mathscr{C}$ is a subspace of $V$ if and only if one of the elements of $\mathscr{C}$ contains all the other elements.

Proof: We only need to prove that the union of subspaces is a subspace then one must contain the others. We proceed by induction. Let $\mathscr{C}=\{U_1,U_2,\cdots ,U_n \}$. The case when $n=1$ is trivial. Suppose the theorem holds for $n=m$, where $m$ not exceeds $|F|$ when $F$ is finite, or $F$ is infinite, we only need to consider the case when each $U_i$, where $i=1,2,\cdots ,m+1$, has at least one elements not in other subspaces. Without loss of generality, let $x\in U_1$ where $x\notin U_i$ for $i\neq 1$ and $y\in U_2$ where $y\notin U_i$ for $i\neq 2$. Consider the set $$H=\{\alpha x+y|\alpha\in F\}$$ The cardinality of $H$ is equal to $F$, and $H$ is a subset of the union of the elements in $\mathscr{C}$ except $U_1$. By the pigeonhole principle, if $m+1$ not exceed $|F|$ when $F$ is finite, or $F$ is infinite, then there exist two different $\alpha_1 x+y$ and $\alpha_2 x+y$ in one subspace $U_i$ other than $U_1$, thus we have $(\alpha_1-\alpha_2)x\in U_i$, which means $x\in U_i$,but $x\notin U_i$, a contradiction. Therefore, there exists one subspace $U_j$ that is in the union $U_1\cup U_2\cup \cdots \cup U_{j-1}\cup U_{j+1}\cup \cdots \cup U_{m+1}$. But then the union of the elements in $\mathscr{C}\setminus\{U_j\}$ is a subspace of $V$, by induction hypothesis, the theorem holds for $n=m+1$. ▮
